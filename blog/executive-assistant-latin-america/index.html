How to Hire a Remote Executive Assistant from Latin America (2026 Guide)

March 29, 2026 8 min read By Placibly

A great executive assistant changes how a founder or senior executive operates. The right EA eliminates calendar chaos, filters inbound noise, owns communication workflows, and creates leverage where there was none. The wrong one — or no EA at all — means hours of your week disappearing into scheduling, inbox management, and coordination work that should never reach your desk.

Latin America has become the go-to talent pool for US companies hiring remote EAs. The combination of overlapping time zones, strong English skills, and 50-60% cost savings compared to US-based hires makes it one of the clearest hiring decisions a growing company can make. This guide covers exactly how to do it right.

Executive Assistant vs. Virtual Assistant: Know the Difference

The terms get used interchangeably but they represent meaningfully different roles. Understanding the distinction before you hire will save you from a frustrating mismatch.

Virtual Assistant

A VA typically handles a defined set of repeatable tasks: scheduling, data entry, research, basic customer support, social media posting. The work is more transactional, the scope is narrower, and the level of judgment required is lower. A VA is a great hire for someone who needs task execution help.

Executive Assistant

An EA operates at a higher level of trust and complexity. They own your calendar completely, not just manage it. They draft communications on your behalf, make decisions within defined parameters, represent you in external interactions, manage relationships with vendors and partners, and proactively solve problems before they reach you. A great EA thinks ahead, not just behind.

Rule of thumb: If you find yourself explaining the same type of task repeatedly, you need a VA. If you want someone who can anticipate what you need before you ask, you need an EA.

Core Skills to Look for in a LATAM Executive Assistant

Not all strong admin professionals make great EAs. When hiring from Latin America specifically, these are the skills that separate excellent candidates from average ones:

  • Proactive communication: They flag issues before you ask. If a meeting is at risk of running over, they have a solution ready. If an email needs a response and you haven't replied, they follow up.
  • Advanced written English: EAs draft on your behalf. Their written communication should be polished enough that you rarely need to edit what they write.
  • Calendar ownership: Deep familiarity with Google Calendar or Outlook, including scheduling across time zones, buffer time management, and protecting focused work blocks.
  • Discretion: EAs have access to sensitive information — financials, board communications, personnel matters. Trust and discretion are non-negotiable.
  • Tech fluency: Comfortable with your stack. Notion, Slack, HubSpot, Zoom, project management tools — the faster they onboard to your tools, the faster they create value.
  • Problem-solving orientation: When something unexpected comes up, a great EA brings you a solution, not just a description of the problem.

How to Interview for an EA Role

The interview process for an EA must go deeper than a standard admin interview. You are evaluating judgment, not just competence.

Stage 1: Written screening task

Send a realistic scenario before the first call. Examples that work well for EA roles:

  • Draft a response to a difficult email from a client (provide the original email)
  • Look at a sample week of calendar conflicts and propose a resolution
  • Research a vendor and summarize your findings in a format you'd actually use

You are evaluating written English quality, judgment, attention to format, and whether they read the instructions carefully.

Stage 2: 45-minute video interview

Focus less on past responsibilities and more on how they think. Good questions:

  • "Walk me through how you manage a calendar for someone who has back-to-back meetings and no focus time."
  • "Tell me about a time you caught a problem before your executive did. What happened?"
  • "How do you prioritize when three urgent things land at the same time?"
  • "If I don't respond to an email in 48 hours, what do you do?"

Stage 3: Paid trial week

Give the top candidate access to a sanitized version of your calendar and inbox for one week. Assign real tasks. Evaluate quality of output, proactiveness, and communication style before committing to a full hire.

Onboarding Your LATAM EA for Success

The first 30 days determine whether your EA becomes truly valuable or just an expensive scheduler. The investment in a structured onboarding process pays for itself within weeks.

  1. Day 1-3: Document your preferences in detail. How do you like your calendar structured? What are non-negotiables in your schedule (protected mornings, lunch, etc.)? What tone do you prefer in external emails? What does your ideal inbox look like?
  2. Week 1: Shadow everything. They observe your workflow, read through past communications, and ask questions. No independent action yet — only learning.
  3. Week 2: Assisted execution. They draft; you review and edit. They schedule; you confirm. Heavy feedback loop.
  4. Week 3-4: Independent execution on low-stakes tasks. You review selectively. Start delegating one ownership area fully (e.g., all scheduling).
  5. Day 30 review: Formal check-in. What is working? What needs adjustment? Set a 90-day development plan together.

What to Delegate to Your EA (And What Not To)

Excellent tasks to delegate to an EA:

  • Full calendar management and scheduling coordination
  • Inbox triage and drafting replies
  • Travel planning and logistics
  • Meeting preparation: agendas, pre-reads, follow-up notes
  • Vendor and contractor relationship management
  • Expense tracking and reporting
  • Research and competitive intelligence
  • Light project coordination and deadline tracking

What to keep in your hands:

  • Final decisions on hiring, firing, or compensation
  • Strategic communications with board members or investors (drafts are fine; final send is yours)
  • Anything requiring your specific expertise or authority

A useful test: Before sending any task to your EA, ask yourself: "Does this require my unique judgment, or just my name?" If the answer is "just my name," it belongs on your EA's plate.

The Bottom Line

Hiring a remote EA from Latin America is one of the highest-leverage decisions a US executive can make. You recover 10-20 hours per week of your own time, you get real-time responsiveness without overnight delays, and you pay a fraction of what a comparable US-based hire would cost.

The key is hiring for judgment, not just skills. An EA who thinks ahead, communicates proactively, and owns problems end-to-end is worth five times what a task-executor is. The interview process described above is specifically designed to surface that quality of candidate.
